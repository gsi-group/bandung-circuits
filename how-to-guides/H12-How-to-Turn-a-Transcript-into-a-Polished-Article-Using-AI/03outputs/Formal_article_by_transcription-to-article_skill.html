Hands Off Asia: Militarism, Sovereignty, and the Resistance Across the Region

These minutes were generated by AI from the "Hands Off Asia! Peace, Sovereignty, and Anti-US Militarism" webinar (Tricontinental Institute for Social Research & International People's Assembly, 30 April 2025). They have not been human-reviewed and may contain errors.

Fifty-one years ago, on 30 April 1975, a tank crashed through the gates of the Independence Palace in Saigon. The image marked the end of three decades of war and the defeat of the most powerful military in the world by an organised people. The victory did not come easy: over three million Vietnamese dead, entire regions devastated, and the toxic shadow of Agent Orange still poisoning over four million people across Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia. Yet the lesson endures – that even the mightiest empire can be resisted.

The threat the Vietnamese people fought against, however, has not gone away. Today, under its hyper-imperialist phase, the United States maintains an arc of military infrastructure stretching from Japan and South Korea to the Philippines, Guam, Australia, Diego Garcia, and the Gulf states. The US-backed genocide in Gaza grinds into its third year, and the illegal US-Israeli war on Iran has proven in blood that a US military presence does not mean protection for the peoples of the region. It was against this backdrop that the Tricontinental Institute for Social Research and the International People’s Assembly convened a panel of women organisers and scholars from across Asia to launch the Hands Off Asia campaign – a call for the removal of US and NATO military bases from the region, an end to aggressive military pacts, and respect for the sovereignty of Asian peoples and nations.

The Architecture of Containment

The militarisation of the region is accelerating at a pace not seen since 1945. Japan has doubled its military budget and purchased over 400 Tomahawk cruise missiles in the largest remilitarisation since the end of the anti-fascist war. In Taiwan, the United States has sold over $20 billion worth of arms since 2019. South Korea, under pressure from Washington, has agreed to gradually increase defence spending to 3.5 per cent of GDP, while the US has openly raised the possibility that troops stationed there could be drawn into a conflict over Taiwan.

This is not merely an accumulation of hardware; it is the consolidation of a military architecture designed for containment. Chang An Hwang, General Secretary of the International Strategy Center in Seoul, warned that Washington is urging allies to assume a greater share of responsibility for what it terms alliance modernisation. The concept extends beyond the Korean Peninsula. He described the emergence of a kill web – an integrated military network linking South Korea, Japan, and the Philippines into a single system expanding warfare beyond land, sea, and air into space, cyber, and electromagnetic domains. The then-commander of US Forces Korea reportedly described the peninsula as "like a fixed aircraft carrier," while strategic planning documents map a trilateral military triangle aimed at checking China, Russia, and the Democratic People’s Republic of Korea.

In the Philippines, the scale of integration is equally stark. Corazon Valdes-Fabros, co-president of the International Peace Bureau, described Balikatan 2026 – the largest war games in Philippine history – involving 17,000 foreign troops from the US, Japan, Australia, and NATO forces. The exercises are framed as defence, yet for ordinary Filipinos they signal something more sinister: the transformation of their archipelago into a forward tripwire state, positioned as the first line of fire in a conflict it did not choose.

Bases as Targets, Not Shields

The central justification for this buildup – that US bases deter aggression and protect host nations – was dismantled repeatedly throughout the discussion. As Tings Chak, Asia co-coordinator of Tricontinental Institute, put it:

"A US base is not a shield, but in fact a target."

Setareh Sadeqi, speaking from Esfahan in Iran, offered the most immediate proof. During the recent US-Israeli war on Iran, Gulf monarchies provided their airspace and territory to US military assets for attacks on Iranian cities. The bases that had been sold to these regimes as protection became, in the course of the war, legitimate targets of retaliation. Iranian missiles and drones struck US occupation facilities in Qatar, Bahrain, and Saudi Arabia. By Sadeqi’s account, these facilities endured more than 170 attacks, with damage far exceeding what Washington acknowledged. The promise of a security umbrella, she argued, had been exposed as a flawed bargain:

"Security requires distance from the imperialist power, not dependency on the imperialist power."

The same logic applies across the region. In Okinawa, which hosts 70 per cent of US military facilities in Japan on just 0.6 per cent of the country’s land area, residents have been designated the front line of a potential Taiwan contingency. Keiko Yonaha, co-representative of No More Battle of Okinawa, noted that bilateral US-Japan planning explicitly assumes Okinawan residents would receive the counterattack in any conflict – a repetition of the 1945 Battle of Okinawa, in which one in three or four Okinawans were killed. The density of bases in Okinawa is 386 times that of mainland Japan, yet under the Status of Forces Agreement, local authorities are denied access to investigate environmental contamination or hold US personnel accountable for crimes.

Soft Power and Economic Coercion

Military force is only one face of empire. Luna Nguyen, a Marxist-Leninist translator and content creator from Vietnam, emphasised that her country is threatened less by invasion than by the soft power of Western imperialism – tariffs, sanctions, and clandestine political sabotage. After the war, the United States imposed a comprehensive embargo on Vietnam. When the Soviet Union collapsed and Vietnam stood on the verge of nationwide famine, Washington demanded payment of $145 million in debt accrued by the fascist puppet regime in the south – compensation, in effect, for the very money and weapons used to massacre Vietnamese civilians. Vietnam paid until just seven years ago.

To secure stability, Vietnam developed what is known as the Four No’s policy: no military alliances, no alignment with one country against another, no foreign military bases on Vietnamese soil, and no use of force in international relations. The policy, Luna argued, has been a resounding success in protecting national independence while avoiding entanglement in the conflicts stoked by the West.

Chen Meixian, a professor at National Cheng Kung University in Taiwan, traced a parallel history of ideological control. From 1950 to 1979, the US maintained direct military bases on the island while backing a dictatorship that enforced four decades of martial-law repression. After diplomatic normalisation with the People’s Republic of China compelled the withdrawal of formal troops in 1979, the ideological apparatus remained. Through educational programmes, media influence, and fellowship foundations, the US systematically cultivated pro-American elites. The result, Chen argued, is a mainstream ideology that is pro-American, anti-communist, and anti-China – a form of ideological occupation that affects every person on the island.

The economic dimension is equally extractive. Corazon Valdes-Fabros warned of Pax Siliconica – a US high-tech manufacturing zone carved out of 4,000 acres in the Philippines, granted extraterritoriality for up to 99 years and governed by US common law. Within its walls operate not only semiconductor firms but Lockheed Martin, the world’s largest defence contractor. The zone is designed to exploit Philippine critical minerals and labour to feed the US war supply chain – what Valdes-Fabros described as "occupation dressed up as progress."

The Everyday Costs

The footprint of empire is measured not only in missiles and treaties but in poisoned water, stolen land, and impunity for violence. In Okinawa, PFAS contamination of drinking water continues to threaten public health, yet the US-Japan Status of Forces Agreement blocks independent investigation. Okinawan women’s groups have documented sexual assaults by US military personnel from 1945 to the present, including the rape and murder of a five-year-old girl in 1945, a nine-month-old infant in 1949, and a fifth-grade student in 1996. Most offenders are acquitted or flee to the United States, beyond the reach of local law.

In the Philippines, the war economy bleeds into daily survival. Inflation stands at 4.1 per cent, while rice – the national staple – hovers at 60 to 70 pesos per kilo. The closure of the Strait of Hormuz during the West Asia war endangered nearly two million overseas Filipino workers in the Middle East. As Valdes-Fabros noted:

"True security is not missiles. It is food on the table. It is a roof over our head and communities that thrive in peace."

South Korea bears a similar burden. Approximately 28,500 US troops occupy 95 million square metres of land – equivalent to over 13,000 football pitches – across 62 military bases. The crimes committed by US personnel, including murder and sexual assault, have gone largely unpunished under the Status of Forces Agreement. Meanwhile, the alliance’s modernisation threatens to erode what remains of South Korean autonomy in foreign and security policy, dragging the peninsula deeper into a great-power rivalry that undermines the prospect of inter-Korean peace and reunification.

Resistance and Solidarity

Against this architecture of bases and dependency, the panel traced a long and living tradition of resistance. In 1952, nearly 470 delegates from 50 countries gathered in Beijing to demand an end to US bases and the rearmament of Japan. Okinawan farmers and women have resisted land seizures by bayonets and bulldozers since the 1950s. Pacific Islanders built the nuclear-free and independent Pacific movement. Filipinos voted to remove US military bases in the 1990s. These histories are not distant; they are the foundation on which contemporary organising rests.

Vietnam’s Four No’s policy was offered not as a unique national quirk but as a formula adaptable across the region.

"Every nation in Asia should come together to push the United States military and intelligence apparatus out of our region," Luna Nguyen argued. "We must not allow ourselves to be further divided and weaponised against one another for the benefit of the empire."

The Hands Off Asia campaign emerges from this matrix of shared experience. Its demand is straightforward: remove US and NATO military bases, end aggressive military pacts, and respect Asian sovereignty. But its vision, the panellists insisted, is broader. It is a world where scientific capacity serves renewable energy rather than missile guidance, where workers build schools rather than ammunition hubs, and where solidarity replaces strategic dependency.

In their closing remarks, the speakers returned to the practical work of organising. Study the economic base of imperialism, Luna urged; understand that sanctions and tariffs are as destructive as bombs. Find indigenous frameworks of resistance, Setareh Sadeqi added; Iran’s refusal to surrender to imperialist demands draws on cultural traditions of dignity and justice as much as on military capability. Build dialogue across borders, Keiko Yonaha and Chen Meixian insisted; the first step to peace is understanding. Take to the streets, said Chang An Hwang; international solidarity must be mobilised into collective action. And learn from those on the front lines, Corazon Valdes-Fabros concluded; their stories are where the inspiration lives.

The webinar closed with a quote from Ho Chi Minh, spoken six decades ago but revived as the panel’s shared pledge: nothing is more precious than independence and freedom. For the peoples of Asia today, that independence means independence from US military aggression and imperialism – a liberation still unfinished, and still worth fighting for.
