# HANDS OFF ASIA! How US Military Bases Became Targets, Not Shields

These minutes were generated by AI from the "Hands Off Asia! Peace, Sovereignty, and Anti-US Militarism" webinar (Tricontinental Institute for Social Research & International People's Assembly, 30 April 2025). They have not been human-reviewed and may contain errors.

## I. April 30, 1975: The Tank and the Lesson

At 10:45 AM on April 30, 1975, a North Vietnamese tank crashed through the gates of Saigon's Independence Palace. The photograph became iconic: a Soviet-built T-54 flattening wrought iron, soldiers in pith helmets raising the flag of the National Liberation Front. The war was over.

Fifty-one years later, the Tricontinental Institute for Social Research hosted a webinar on the anniversary. The timing was deliberate. "The threat that the Vietnamese people fought against has not gone away," said chair Tings Chak. If anything, it has metastasized.

From 1965 to 1972, the United States sent nearly three million troops to Vietnam. By the war's end, over two million Vietnamese civilians and one million soldiers were dead. The US dropped more bombs on Vietnam than all sides dropped in World War II. And then there was Agent Orange—dioxin-laden defoliant sprayed across 4.5 million acres. Today there are over four million Agent Orange victims in Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia. The US has never apologized.

But this article is not about the war you know. It is about the wars that followed—the quiet ones, the economic ones, the ones still being fought through military bases that stretch from Japan to the Persian Gulf like a noose.

## II. The Debt Trap: Vietnam's Post-War Punishment

Luna Nguyen, a translator and content creator from Vietnam, was seven years old when her country made its final payment to the United States. The date was 2018. The amount was $145 million.

Here is how a defeated empire extracts tribute from its victims.

After 1975, the US imposed a comprehensive embargo on Vietnam. No trade. No credit. Nothing. By the late 1980s, Vietnam was on the verge of nationwide famine. The Soviet Union, its primary ally, was collapsing. In 1986, Hanoi launched Đổi Mới—a renovation program that opened the economy to survive. To secure IMF loans, Vietnam accepted privatization and market reforms.

But normalization with the US came with a condition: Vietnam had to pay off debts incurred by the South Vietnamese puppet regime—the very debts that had financed the weapons used to massacre Vietnamese civilians.

"We had to pay millions of dollars to compensate them for the very money and weapons which were used to massacre Vietnamese civilians during the war," Nguyen explained. "Vietnam had no choice but to accept the terms of the empire."

The debt took over 20 years to pay off. Vietnam was writing checks to Washington until 2018.

This is the template. The war ends, but the economic warfare continues. The US spent billions backing NGOs, media outlets, and "color revolution" attempts in Vietnam from 2014 to 2019. The goal, Nguyen said, is unchanged: "The USA would love nothing more than for the socialist government of Vietnam to collapse."

## III. Vietnam's Four No's: A Sovereignty Playbook

Vietnam survived by developing what it calls the "Four No's" policy—a framework of diplomatic neutrality refined since the 1990s:

1. No military alliances.
2. No alignment with one country against another.
3. No foreign military bases on Vietnamese territory.
4. No use of force or threats in international relations.

The third "No" is the one that matters most. The US has been begging Vietnam for years to allow naval bases on its coastline. The answer is always no. "Modern history," Nguyen noted, "especially the US aggression against Iran, clearly shows that if there are foreign military bases on our territory, it is easy to turn our country into the target of hostile forces."

Vietnam learned what other nations are now discovering: a US base is not a shield. It is a target. It is a tripwire. It is a forfeiture of the host nation's ability to de-escalate.

## IV. The Iran War: Bases as Bull's-Eyes

Setareh Sadeqi spoke from Esfahan, Iran's third-largest city. It was two weeks after the ceasefire with Israel and the US. "We've got a break from the fighter jets flying over us and hearing explosions during days and nights," she said. "Anxiously wondering if our loved ones are okay."

The war revealed something the Persian Gulf monarchies had refused to believe. For decades, the US sold them a protection racket: host our bases, buy our weapons, and we will keep you safe from Iran. The price was steep. Saudi Arabia, UAE, Bahrain, Kuwait, and Qatar spent billions on US defense systems and granted access to Al Udeid, the Fifth Fleet headquarters, Prince Sultan Air Base, and dozens of other facilities.

When the war came, those bases became targets. Not collateral damage. Primary targets.

"Iran knows which bases and which air spaces have been used in order to attack," Sadeqi said. Over 170 attacks hit US facilities during the conflict. The "invincible" Patriot missile systems failed. The bases that were supposed to deter war instead imported war into host countries.

"US bases do not simply deter conflict," Sadeqi explained. "They also import conflict. A conflict that is outside of these countries becomes part of those countries."

The Gulf states discovered they had traded sovereignty for vulnerability. By embedding themselves into Washington's military architecture, they lost diplomatic maneuverability. They became "forward positions" and "high-value targets." The protection they purchased was an illusion.

"The promised shield—in form of security guarantees and rapid response and deterrence—actually becomes bull's eyes," Sadeqi said. Fixed facilities are visible targets for missiles and drones. And drones, as Iran demonstrated, are affordable, numerous, and capable of bypassing expensive defense systems.

## V. Okinawa: 386 Times the Density

Keiko Yonaha's home is 0.6% of Japan's land area. It hosts 70% of US military facilities in Japan. That is a density 386 times higher than the rest of the country.

This concentration was not accidental. It was colonial.

Okinawa was the only Japanese territory where a ground war was fought in World War II. One in three Okinawans died in the Battle of Okinawa. After the war, the 1951 peace treaty included secret pacts: the US could maintain bases anywhere in Japan, and in a crisis, Japanese forces would be under US command. Article Three placed Okinawa under direct US military occupation until 1972.

Forced land seizures began in 1950, before the treaty was even signed. Farmers were driven from their homes by bayonets and bulldozers. When mainland Japan developed anti-base movements in the 1950s, the US simply moved its facilities to Okinawa—where the occupied population had no political power to resist.

The consequences are documented in a report by Okinawan Women Act Against Military Violence. From 1945 to 2024, US military personnel committed sexual assaults including the rape and murder of a 5-year-old girl in 1945, a 9-month-old baby in 1949, and a fifth-grader by three soldiers in 1995. Most offenders are acquitted. Convicted criminals can flee to the US. The Status of Forces Agreement blocks investigations and environmental testing.

Then came December 24, 2021. The US and Japanese governments informed Okinawa that it would be the "front line" in a potential Taiwan contingency. The plan is called EABO—Expeditionary Advanced Base Operations. In practice, it means Okinawan civilians would absorb the counterattack in any conflict.

"We had already learned that the military did not protect the residents during the Battle of Okinawa," Yonaha said. "That kind of history will be repeated."

## VI. Taiwan: Ideological Occupation

Chen Meixian, a professor at National Cheng Kung University, started with geography. Thirty thousand years ago, Taiwan and mainland China were connected by a land bridge. Chinese state activity in Taiwan dates back 1,800 years. Migration of Han Chinese began 900 years ago. Today 96% of Taiwan's population is Han Chinese.

But history, she argued, has been obscured by ideology.

From 1950 to 1979, the US maintained military bases in Taiwan. The Seventh Fleet patrolled the Taiwan Strait. A mutual defense treaty locked Taiwan into the anti-communist Cold War front. Meanwhile, the Kuomintang regime imposed martial law and "White Terror" for 40 years, executing thousands of leftists and independence activists with US support.

Then came the more subtle occupation. After 1979, when the US established diplomatic relations with Beijing and withdrew formal troops from Taiwan, the control continued through "ideological manipulation."

"US has systematic ideological propaganda machine," Chen explained. Educational curriculum, media ownership, fellowship programs (like the East-West Center where Chen herself studied). The themes are consistent: anti-communist liberalism, modernization theory, Western democracy as universal, anti-China sentiment.

The result: "Taiwan's mainstream ideology is pro-American, admire America, anti-communist, anti-China."

The weapons sales continued—$20 billion since 2019, including a recent forced purchase of $400 billion in arms. US troops still train Taiwanese forces. The Taiwan Relations Act (1979) maintains the military relationship despite the diplomatic shift.

Chen calls this "a type of colonization"—not formal occupation, but control through narrative.

## VII. Philippines: Tripwire State

Corazon Valdes-Fabros described the current Balikatan 2026 exercises: 17,000 foreign troops from the US, Japan, Australia, and NATO conducting the largest war games in Philippine history. This in a country whose constitution explicitly prohibits foreign military bases.

The Philippines is being turned into a "forward tripwire state," Valdes-Fabros argued. Northern Luzon now hosts a massive fuel depot near the Taiwan Strait. Southern bases store ammunition and oil. The former US naval base at Subic Bay is becoming an ammunition hub again.

Then there is "Pax Siliconica"—4,000 acres in the central Philippines carved out for a US-controlled "high-tech manufacturing zone." The lease runs 99 years, renewable. The zone operates under US common law with "diplomatic immunity." Companies include Intel, Micron, and Lockheed Martin—the world's largest defense contractor.

"This is not innovation," Valdes-Fabros said. "It is occupation dressed up as progress." The zone exploits critical minerals and semiconductors to feed the US war supply chain, using cheap Philippine labor.

Meanwhile, inflation in the Philippines hit 4.1% in March 2026. Rice—a basic staple—costs 60 to 70 pesos per kilo. "Why are we paying these prices?" Valdes-Fabros asked. "Because militarism and peace are inextricably linked to our livelihoods."

The trade-off is stark: interoperability of missiles versus interoperability of healthcare systems. Billions for joint operations while classrooms and hospital beds go underfunded.

## VIII. South Korea: The Fixed Aircraft Carrier

Chang-woon Hwang described a recent remark by General Javier Branson, commander of US Forces Korea. He called Korea a "fixed aircraft carrier."

There are 28,500 US troops in South Korea, using 62 bases across 95 million square meters of land—equivalent to over 13,000 soccer fields. The Korea-US Mutual Defense Treaty (1953) and Status of Forces Agreement (1966) formalized this presence.

But "modernization" is changing the mission. The US now openly discusses using Korean-based troops in a potential Taiwan conflict. Washington wants Seoul to increase defense spending to 3.5% of GDP. The "Kill Web" concept would integrate South Korean, Japanese, and Philippine militaries into a single network aimed at China.

In August 2024, Donald Trump demanded that the US be given ownership of the land where its bases sit—not just free use, but sovereign territory with extraterritorial rights. While unrealistic, the demand reveals the trajectory: strategic flexibility for US forces is a "non-negotiable red line."

The risk is clear. "South Korea could effectively become a forward operating base against China," Hwang said. This would increase the risk of being drawn into conflict and harm inter-Korean peace efforts. The peninsula becomes a tripwire for a war Koreans did not choose.

## IX. The Resistance: From Analysis to Solidarity

In the closing roundtable, speakers reflected on what comes next. The consensus was that understanding must lead to organization.

Luna Nguyen emphasized studying Marxism-Leninism to understand the economic base of imperialism. "It's all about the capital, it's all about the money, the natural resources," she said. "It's the true answer of all of that." She offered a free e-book—Vietnam's Marxism-Leninism curriculum, translated for international readers.

Setareh Sadeqi stressed indigenous resistance models. "Iran has made it clear that they're not going to negotiate at gunpoint," she said. The slogan "Never to humiliation" reflects Shia theological values of justice and dignity—not exportable ideology, but locally rooted defiance.

Keiko Yonaha emphasized dialogue across divides. The Okinawa-Taiwan Dialogue Project (2021–2023) brought together activists from different political backgrounds to find common ground on peace. "Through that dialogue, first of all, we could understand each other. This is the first step to build up peace."

Chen Meixian focused on class analysis. "Different classes of people are impacted or influenced by all the military bases or the ideological control," she noted. Solidarity must bridge class divides, not just national borders.

Corazon Valdes-Fabros returned to history. Vietnam had politicized her generation in the 1970s. Today's crisis, she argued, can politicize a new generation—if organizers translate analysis into sustained connection. "It's sometimes not actually the inside the classroom that we learn a lot. It could be in the pizza line. In the peace camp."

Chang-woon Hwang closed with practical organizing: "We have to think about how can we deliver this information, our experience to a general public... mobilize people into the street, into the events that we can talk about international solidarity."

## X. The Sovereignty Alternative

The webinar ended with a quote from Ho Chi Minh: "Nothing is more precious than independence and freedom."

The speakers were clear that independence means something specific in 2026. It means no foreign military bases. It means rejecting the protection racket that transforms host countries into targets. It means understanding that US military spending—$954 billion, one-third of the global total—is theft from healthcare, education, and climate resilience.

Vietnam's Four No's offer one model. Iran's refusal to surrender offers another. Okinawa's decades of resistance, Taiwan's struggle against ideological control, the Philippines' fight against remilitarization, and South Korea's organizing against the "Kill Web"—each represents a front in the same conflict.

The Gulf monarchies are learning late what Vietnam learned early: security requires distance from imperial power, not dependency on it. The bases do not protect. They import conflict. They foreclose diplomatic options. They make host nations complicit in crimes against their neighbors.

As the webinar closed, speakers and audience chanted in unison: "Hands off Asia."
