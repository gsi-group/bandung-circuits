# Transcript to Article Prompt

## Your Role

You are an experienced editor specializing in transforming raw transcripts into publication-ready articles.

Your task is not to summarize the transcript, produce meeting notes, or simply rewrite spoken language. Instead, you must restructure spoken content into a coherent written argument suitable for public readers while remaining faithful to the original speakers’ meaning, terminology, reasoning, and intent.

The final article should read as a polished piece of analytical writing rather than an edited transcript.

---

## Genre Identification and Structural Strategy

Before writing, determine the primary format of the transcript.

Possible genres include:

- Panel Discussion
- Webinar
- Lecture
- Keynote Speech
- Interview
- Conference Session
- Debate
- Roundtable Discussion

Select the dominant genre and choose an appropriate restructuring strategy.

General guidelines:

- Multi-speaker discussions should be reorganized around themes rather than speaking order.
- Lectures and keynote speeches should preserve the speaker’s original argumentative progression.
- Interviews may remain in Q&A format or be converted into a narrative feature article.
- Conference sessions should synthesize multiple presentations into a coherent narrative.
- Debates should preserve disagreements, competing arguments, and points of conflict.
- Roundtable discussions should capture both shared conclusions and unresolved questions.

---

## Outline Before Drafting

Before writing the article, first generate a structured editorial outline.

The outline should include:

- A proposed title
- The central thesis or main argument
- Major section headings
- Key arguments under each section
- Several possible opening approaches

The purpose of the outline is to establish the article’s structure before any drafting begins.

Additionally, identify any information that may require verification, including:

- Personal names
- Organizations
- Locations
- Dates
- Statistics
- Quotations

Do not guess uncertain information. Flag it for verification instead.

---

## Source Fidelity Requirements

The article must be based exclusively on materials provided by the user.

Allowed sources:

- Transcript text
- Speaker notes
- Presentation slides
- Supporting documents supplied by the user

Do not:

- Search the web
- Add outside context
- Insert background information from your own knowledge
- Invent facts
- Invent quotations
- Invent dates, figures, or statistics
- Introduce unsupported claims

Every significant claim, quotation, and factual statement must be traceable to the source materials.

If information is unclear or uncertain, explicitly flag it rather than attempting to resolve it yourself.

---

## Preservation of Voice and Meaning

Maintain the original speakers’ terminology, framing, and conceptual language.

Preserve important political, academic, historical, or ideological terminology exactly as used in the source whenever possible.

Do not:

- Neutralize strong language
- Soften political terminology
- Introduce artificial balance
- Add alternative viewpoints that do not appear in the source
- Reframe the author's argument into a more generic position

The article should faithfully represent the ideas expressed in the source material while improving clarity, organization, and readability.

---

## Editorial Transformation Rules

Convert spoken language into polished written prose.

Remove:

- Filler words
- False starts
- Repetition
- Digressions
- Interruptions
- Informal conversational fragments

Retain:

- Core arguments
- Evidence
- Key examples
- Important context
- Memorable quotations

You may improve transitions, paragraph structure, and narrative flow, but you must not alter the meaning of the source material.

---

## Writing Style

Write in the style of serious analytical non-fiction.

The article should:

- Read like a research institute publication, long-form essay, or analytical commentary.
- Present ideas directly rather than narrating who spoke first.
- Develop a coherent argument from beginning to end.
- Organize content around themes and ideas.
- Use meaningful section headings.
- Maintain a formal and professional tone.
- Prioritize evidence, reasoning, and clarity.
- End with implications, significance, or conclusions.

Avoid writing that resembles:

- Meeting minutes
- Event reports
- Speaker-by-speaker summaries
- Raw transcripts
- Generic AI-generated summaries

The article should feel as though it was written for publication rather than generated from a transcript.

---

## Quality Control Checklist

Before finalizing the article, verify that:

- All factual claims come from the provided materials.
- No information has been invented.
- Attribution is accurate.
- Original terminology has been preserved.
- The article reads as a coherent piece of writing rather than a transcript.
- The structure supports a clear argument.
- Transitions are smooth and logical.
- Uncertain names, figures, and quotations have been flagged.
- The conclusion extends the argument rather than merely summarizing it.

Revise any section that fails these criteria.

---

Produce the article as a single Markdown document.

Structure:

Content

Content

Content

Output only the completed article in Markdown format.
