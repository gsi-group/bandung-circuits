# Voice & Fidelity Rules (non-negotiable)

These transcripts are the real words and arguments of activists, organisers, and
researchers — often from Global South movements and institutes. Fidelity here is
not stylistic preference; misrepresenting them is reputationally and politically
serious. Apply every rule below in both the outline and the draft.

## A. Do not invent (closed-book on the transcript)

- The article is **closed-book**: build it only from the transcript and any
  materials the user explicitly hands you. Do not browse the web, search, or draw
  on your own background knowledge to add or "verify" content — that is the main
  source of hallucination. If the user explicitly asks for outside research,
  label clearly what came from outside vs. from the transcript.
- Never add facts, statistics, dates, names, events, or claims not in the transcript.
- "Not stated" is not an invitation to infer. If a logical bridge is missing, write
  around it or flag it — do not fabricate the connective tissue as fact.
- Do not invent quotes. A quotation mark means those were the speaker's words.

## B. Flag, don't guess (the ⚠ Verify list)

ASR mangles proper nouns from many languages — names of people, organisations,
places, and numbers are the highest-risk items. In the outline stage, list every
one you are not confident about and ask the user to verify. Examples of what to flag:
- Personal names and titles (especially non-English names).
- Organisation and campaign names, acronyms.
- Place names, regions.
- Statistics, dates, monetary figures, percentages ("386 times the density").
- Any quote you'd attribute to a specific person.

**Check supporting materials first.** If the user supplied slides, a script, or
speaker notes, resolve uncertain names/terms/figures against those before flagging
— prepared materials are usually right where ASR is wrong. Only items that the
supporting materials cannot resolve go on the ⚠ Verify list.

Never silently "correct" a name to a plausible guess from your own knowledge. If
the supporting materials don't settle it, flag it.

## C. Never neutralise the politics

Generic AI defaults sand down political content. Do NOT do any of the following
unless the user explicitly asks:

- **Do not soften terminology.** Keep "imperialism", "settler-colonialism",
  "occupation", "sovereignty", "the Global South", "comrade", "liberation",
  "resistance", "genocide", "apartheid" etc. when the speakers used them. Do not
  swap them for euphemisms like "geopolitical tension", "the conflict", "the situation".
- **Do not inject false balance.** If the speakers made a one-sided political
  argument, the article carries that argument. Do not add "critics say" /
  "on the other hand" framing that wasn't in the source.
- **Do not hedge or both-sides** a clear position into neutrality.
- **Do not scrub named actors** — states, institutions, corporations, individuals
  the speakers held responsible stay named.
- **Preserve the register.** Movement and scholarly registers are different from
  corporate/NGO English; match what the speakers used. Don't NGO-ify.

See `../glossary.md` for the protected-terms list; extend it per project.

## D. Narrative voice & attribution

This is about **the angle of narration**, not just word choice. A finished article
is told in its own voice and makes the argument directly. It is NOT a chronicle of
a meeting.

- **Write in the article's own voice — don't report who-said-what.** The default
  is to state the ideas and build the argument directly, as the article's own
  analysis. A piece about militarisation in Asia should read as an article about
  militarisation in Asia — not "First X discussed this, then Y said that." Framing
  point after point as "the speaker said / X argued / Y noted" keeps it sounding
  like a transcript or minutes, however the speakers are named.
- **Attribution is the exception, used on purpose.** Reach for "X argued…" only
  when it genuinely earns its place: a direct quote, a distinctive or contested
  position, the introduction of a key voice, or where *whose* view it is changes
  the meaning. Everywhere else, present the substance directly and let it stand as
  the article's own claim.
- **How often you attribute depends on the genre.** In a multi-speaker panel or
  discussion you will name people more — distinguishing positions, crediting who
  argued what, and fairly representing several voices is part of the job, so
  "as Vijay argued…", "Setareh countered…" are legitimate and often needed. In a
  single-speaker lecture you need almost none. Either way it stays woven into the
  article's voice, never a roll-call of "first X, then Y".
- **When you do attribute, use the person's name** — full name on first mention,
  then first/last name per the publication ("Vijay argued…") — never "the speaker"
  or "one panellist". For a single-speaker talk, you usually don't need repeated
  attribution at all: it's understood to be their argument (or write it in their
  own first person if publishing it as their essay).
- **Fidelity still holds.** Speaking in the article's own voice never means
  inventing — every idea still comes from the source (closed-book). Never move a
  position to the wrong person, and never merge two distinct positions into one.
- Naming a speaker's organisation or country can situate a claim — use it where it
  adds meaning, not as a routine tag on every sentence.

## E. Faithful rewriting (what you SHOULD do)

- Convert spoken syntax to clean written prose; remove fillers, false starts,
  repetition, and crosstalk.
- Reorder *within the bounds of the genre playbook* for readability.
- Add a lede, transitions, and section framing that serve the reader — these are
  craft, not invention, as long as they introduce no new claims.
- Quote verbatim for the most charged/quotable lines; paraphrase the rest.

## F. Quotes — verbatim, with context

- A quotation mark means those were the speaker's exact words. If you tidy a
  spoken line for readability, it becomes a paraphrase, not a quote — don't keep
  the quotation marks.
- Quote sparingly and for impact: the sharp, charged, or precisely-formulated
  lines. Paraphrase the rest into the article's prose.
- Every quote needs **context**: who said it, in what capacity/organisation, and
  what it responds to — so the reader (and a fact-checker) can situate it.
- **Across languages**, a quote may be rendered in the article's language —
  translate it faithfully and do NOT add labels like "(translated from X)". See
  the Language policy in `SKILL.md`.
- Keep a quotable-lines shortlist from the ingest pass so the draft draws from
  the strongest material rather than whatever was nearest.

## G. Evidence & claims — know what kind of claim you're carrying

As you read, note the *type* of support behind each significant claim — it
changes how you should present it:

- **Cited / sourced** (a report, a figure, a document) — carry it with its source.
- **Logical argument** — reconstruct the reasoning, don't just assert the conclusion.
- **Appeal to authority** (a named theorist/leader) — attribute it as such.
- **Anecdotal / experiential** — present it as testimony, not as proof.

A figure or fact stated once, in passing, and not sourced is **thin**: surface it
in the outline's Gaps note so the user can cut, soften, or supplement it. Never
upgrade a thin claim into a confident factual assertion in the draft.

## H. When unsure

If you cannot tell whether something is faithful — what a speaker meant, who said
it, whether a number is right — surface it to the user rather than resolving it
silently in the draft.
