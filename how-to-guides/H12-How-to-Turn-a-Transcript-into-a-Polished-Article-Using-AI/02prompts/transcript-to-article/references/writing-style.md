# Writing Style — the prose target

The article should read like rigorous, politically committed analytical
non-fiction: the register of a serious research institute or movement
publication, not a blog post and not a corporate report. The aim is to educate
and persuade through evidence and argument, from a clear standpoint, in prose a
general but intelligent reader can follow.

Use this as the default. If the user supplies a style sample, that sample wins.

## Voice & stance

- **Tell it as an article, not a report of a talk.** Write in the article's own
  voice and make the argument directly — don't narrate "X said… then Y said…".
  Explicit attribution ("X argued…") is the exception, reserved for quotes,
  distinctive/contested positions, or introducing a key voice. See
  `voice-and-fidelity.md`, D — this is the difference between a finished article
  and something that still reads like a transcript.
- **Committed, not neutral.** The piece argues from a standpoint (the speakers'
  standpoint) — typically anti-imperialist, pro-sovereignty, in solidarity with
  Global South / popular struggles. Do not manufacture "balance" the source
  didn't have (see `voice-and-fidelity.md`, C).
- **Collective voice where appropriate.** A "we" voice can carry the shared
  political project (e.g. of an institute or movement), if it fits the source and
  the publication. Don't force first person onto a multi-speaker panel.
- **Authority through evidence.** Persuasion rests on named specifics —
  people, places, organisations, dates, figures, events, policies — not on
  adjectives. Prefer the concrete fact to the sweeping claim.

## Structure of the argument

- **Open with stakes or history, not housekeeping.** A strong piece usually
  begins with a historical anchor, a concrete scene, or the central problem —
  then states what's at stake — rather than "This webinar brought together…".
- **Develop, don't list.** Move context → analysis → implications / what is to
  be done. Each section should advance the argument, not just record a speaker.
- **Use binary oppositions as analytical scaffolding** where the material
  supports them: North/South, core/periphery, capital/labour, occupation/
  sovereignty, dependency/self-determination. They clarify the political logic.
- **Thematic sections with clear headings.** Headings name ideas
  ("Bases as targets, not shields"), not agenda items ("Speaker 3").
- **Close with consequence.** Conclusions draw out implications, challenges, or a
  call to action — they don't merely summarise.

## Openings (offer 2–3 at the outline stage)

Draft alternative ledes and let the user choose:
- **Historical anchor** — a date/event that frames the whole piece.
- **Concrete scene or image** — a specific moment that embodies the argument.
- **Sharp claim / stakes** — the central thesis stated plainly up front.
Evaluate each for: specificity, the promise it makes the reader, and fit with the
publication's voice. Avoid generic "In today's world…" openers.

## Sentences & diction

- **Formal register; avoid colloquialism.** No "kind of", "a lot of", "stuff".
- **Vary sentence length deliberately.** Complex, subordinated sentences carry
  layered analysis ("while…", "although…", "since…"); short declaratives land
  the point. Don't let every sentence be long — rhythm aids argument.
- **Use the field's terminology precisely** — hegemony, dependency, accumulation,
  sovereignty, super-exploitation, neoliberalism, multipolarity — when the
  material warrants it. Don't dilute it; don't pad with it either.
- **Nominalisation for processes** (the erosion of, the consolidation of, the
  emergence of) where it lets you treat an action as a concept in the argument.
- **Modality marks stance** — "must", "cannot", "should", "may" signal necessity,
  impossibility, recommendation. Use deliberately.
- **Figurative language sparingly** — an occasional precise metaphor to make a
  concept vivid ("the footprint of empire"), never decoration for its own sake.

## Mechanics (house conventions)

- **En dash (–), not em dash (—)** for parenthetical breaks. This also helps the
  prose not read as AI-generated.
- **Proper nouns, technical terms, and domain concepts: roman type, Title Case —
  not italics** (Global South, the Non-Aligned Movement, structural adjustment).
- **Italics only for** non-English foreign terms (*Nuestra América*, *mística*,
  *militante*) and, very rarely, genuine rhetorical emphasis with no better device.
- **Lists** (bulleted/numbered) only where they genuinely aid clarity; prose is
  the default for an article.
- **Spelling:** match the publication. Default to British spelling (organise,
  mobilisation, programme, defence, labour) for institute/movement publications;
  switch to US if the user/sample indicates it.
- **Cohesion** via logical connectors (therefore, however, furthermore,
  meanwhile) and deliberate repetition of key concepts.

## Quality checklist (run at Stage 3 before handing back)

- [ ] Every factual claim, figure, and quote is traceable to the transcript.
- [ ] No invented content; no neutralised/softened political terminology.
- [ ] Attribution is correct — no position assigned to the wrong speaker.
- [ ] Told in the article's own voice, not as "X said… then Y said…"; attribution
      is the exception, not the frame. Check the opening especially. No "the speaker".
- [ ] Uncertain names/numbers were flagged, not silently guessed.
- [ ] The opening earns attention and states the stakes; no generic lede.
- [ ] Sections are thematic and advance an argument; headings name ideas.
- [ ] Transitions are smooth; the argument has a through-line.
- [ ] Sentence rhythm varies; register is formal and consistent.
- [ ] En dashes (not em); proper-noun/italics conventions followed.
- [ ] AI tells removed (rule-of-three padding, "it's not just X, it's Y",
      empty intensifiers, over-hedging) — consider a de-AI/humanising pass if available.
- [ ] Conclusion draws out consequence, not just summary.
