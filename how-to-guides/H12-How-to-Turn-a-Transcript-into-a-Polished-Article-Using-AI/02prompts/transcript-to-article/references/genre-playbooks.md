# Genre Playbooks

Each transcript genre needs a *different* restructuring strategy. Detect the
primary genre, confirm with the user, then apply that playbook. A transcript can
be a hybrid (e.g. lecture + Q&A) — pick the dominant form and note the secondary.

---

## 1. Panel / Webinar (multiple speakers, one event)

**Signals:** 3+ distinct speakers; a chair/moderator; speakers introduced in turn;
often country- or theme-by-speaker; opening/closing remarks; campaign or event framing.

**Strategy — THEMATIC RE-CLUSTERING (do not follow speaking order).**
Speakers raise overlapping points at different moments. Pull those points out and
regroup them into thematic chapters that build an argument for the reader.
Example: instead of Vietnam→Iran→Okinawa→…, organise as "Lessons from anti-colonial
struggle", "The expansion of militarisation", "Bases as targets, not shields", etc.

**Attribution:** tell it in the article's own voice — present the ideas directly,
not as "first X spoke, then Y spoke" (see voice-and-fidelity D). Name people where
it earns its place: a quote, a distinctive position, or introducing a key voice
("As Vijay argued…"). Movement writing *does* name the comrades and organisations —
so attribute on purpose, but don't turn the piece into a roll-call of who said what.

**Pitfalls:** uneven emphasis (one speaker dominating the draft because they spoke
longest); losing a speaker entirely; merging two speakers' distinct positions.

---

## 2. Lecture / Keynote (single speaker, sustained argument)

**Signals:** one dominant voice; a developed thesis; sections that build on each
other; few or no interruptions.

**Strategy — PRESERVE THE ARGUMENTATIVE ARC.** Do *not* shatter it into themes.
Follow the speaker's own line of reasoning; tighten and clarify it. The job is to
turn spoken argument into clean written argument, keeping the speaker's logic and
sequence intact.

**Attribution:** single byline — it's understood to be their argument, so you
usually need little or no "X said" framing at all; just make the argument in the
article's voice. Name them on first mention; first person may suit publishing it
as their essay (confirm with the user). Never "the speaker".

**Pitfalls:** flattening a sharp argument into a neutral survey; reordering steps
and breaking the logic; dropping the speaker's framing or signature concepts.

---

## 3. Interview / Q&A (interviewer + respondent)

**Signals:** alternating question/answer; one party mostly asking, one answering.

**Strategy — CHOOSE THE FORM, ask the user:**
- **Keep Q&A format** — clean up each question and answer; good for fidelity and
  for letting the subject speak in their own voice.
- **Prose feature** — melt answers into a narrative with selective direct quotes.
Default to asking; if no answer, keep Q&A for a single interviewee, prose for a
profile-style piece.

**Attribution:** preserve the respondent's voice and exact key phrasings; quote
verbatim for charged/quotable lines, paraphrase the rest.

**Pitfalls:** putting words in the subject's mouth; smoothing away a deliberate
or pointed phrasing; losing the question that gives an answer its context.

---

## 4. Conference session (multiple talks + Q&A)

**Signals:** several back-to-back presentations, possibly different topics, plus an
audience Q&A block.

**Strategy — hybrid.** Treat the talks like a panel (thematic re-clustering OR a
short section per talk if topics are unrelated), and mine the Q&A for the sharpest
clarifications and tensions — fold those into the relevant theme rather than as a
tacked-on "Q&A" section, unless an exchange is itself the story.

**Pitfalls:** the article becoming a flat list of talks; ignoring the Q&A where the
real argument often surfaces.

---

## 5. Debate / Argument (opposing positions)

**Signals:** speakers disagree; rebuttals; "I disagree", "on the contrary",
competing framings of the same question.

**Strategy — PRESERVE THE DIALECTIC.** Do NOT manufacture consensus or "both-sides"
neutrality. Lay out each position clearly, who holds it, and the strongest form of
their reasoning, then the points of clash. If the event reached a conclusion or a
majority view, say so; if it didn't, present the tension honestly.

**Attribution:** essential — the reader must know who argued what. Never collapse
two opposed positions into one voice.

**Pitfalls:** false balance; sanding a genuine disagreement into mush;
editorialising a winner the transcript doesn't support.

---

## 6. Roundtable / Discussion (peers, exploratory)

**Signals:** several equal participants; conversational; building on each other
rather than presenting or debating.

**Strategy — synthesise the collective thinking** into themes, like a panel, but
preserve the sense of a conversation developing an idea. Note where the group
converged and where it left questions open.

**Pitfalls:** losing the dialogic, exploratory quality; over-formalising into
separate "positions" when participants were thinking together.
