# Critical Analysis: Overextending and Unbalancing Russia

## Document Information

**Title:** Overextending and Unbalancing Russia: Assessing the Impact of Cost-Imposing Options  
**Publisher:** RAND Corporation  
**Year:** 2019  
**Document Type:** Research Brief (Summary of RR-3063-A)

---

## Argument 1: Economic Cost-Imposing Measures Are the Most Promising Options

### Argument Summary

The brief argues that economic measures—particularly expanding U.S. energy production and imposing deeper trade/financial sanctions—represent the most effective ways to "extend" Russia because they exploit Russia's fundamental economic vulnerabilities (dependence on energy exports, small GDP relative to the U.S.) with high likelihood of success, high benefits, and relatively low costs/risks.

### Critical Analysis

**Logical Structure:**

- The argument relies on a sound comparative advantage framework: Russia's economy is "comparatively small and highly dependent on energy exports"
- However, the argument contains a potential **circular reasoning** fallacy: it assumes that because Russia is economically vulnerable, economic pressure will achieve strategic objectives—without establishing a clear causal mechanism between economic stress and desired behavioral change
- The brief acknowledges that sanctions require multilateral cooperation for effectiveness, yet simultaneously claims expanding U.S. energy production "does not need multilateral endorsement"—this creates a tension between unilateral action feasibility and the acknowledged need for coordinated pressure

**Evidentiary Foundation:**

- The assessment tables are based on "qualitative" assessment by the report's authors—no quantitative modeling or historical case analysis is presented
- The claim that expanding U.S. energy production would "limit Russian revenue" assumes static demand and supply responses; it doesn't address how Russia might adapt through cost-cutting, market diversification, or OPEC+ coordination
- The brief cites "oil and gas prices well below peak" as a current vulnerability but doesn't establish that U.S. production increases would depress prices below Russia's break-even point or sustain such depression long enough to force strategic recalculation

**Rhetorical Clarity:**

- The color-coded assessment tables (green/yellow/red) create an appearance of analytical precision that masks the subjective nature of the judgments
- The note explaining the inverted color scheme for costs/risks is buried and could confuse readers

**Argumentative Resilience:**

- **Unaddressed counterargument:** If economic pressure alone were sufficient to compel Russian strategic recalculation, existing sanctions (post-2014) should have already produced measurable behavioral changes—they have not
- The brief doesn't engage with historical evidence that economic pressure often strengthens authoritarian regimes by enabling them to frame scarcity as external aggression requiring national unity

### Vulnerability Rating: 6/10

### Improvement Path

- Include historical case studies of successful/unsuccessful economic coercion of Russia or comparable states
- Model price elasticity scenarios for Russian energy exports under increased U.S. production
- Address the "sanctions fatigue" problem: why would multilateral sanctions be more effective now than previously?

## Argument 2: Military Posturing Can Cost-Effectively Induce Russian Overextension

### Argument Summary

The brief argues that repositioning bombers within striking range, investing in autonomous/remotely piloted aircraft, and developing longer-range strike capabilities can induce Russia to divert resources into costly countermeasures without proportionate U.S. investment.

### Critical Analysis

**Logical Structure:**

- The argument assumes a **responsive adversary**: that Russia will feel compelled to match U.S. capabilities rather than adopting asymmetric responses
- This commits the **mirror-imaging fallacy**—assuming Russia will respond as the U.S. would to similar stimuli
- The brief itself acknowledges this risk partially: "Russia is not seeking military parity with the United States and, thus, might simply choose not to respond to some U.S. military actions"

**Evidentiary Foundation:**

- The claim that "reposturing bombers...has a high likelihood of success" is asserted without historical precedent or operational analysis
- No evidence is provided that Russian defense planning responds incrementally to U.S. posture changes in the way the model assumes
- The assessment of "low" costs/risks for bomber repositioning appears to ignore the potential for miscalculation, crisis instability, or unintended escalation

**Rhetorical Clarity:**

- The distinction between "high likelihood of extending Russia" and actually achieving strategic objectives is blurred throughout—getting Russia to spend more on defense is treated as synonymous with reducing its threat capability
- The phrase "get Russia to extend itself in strategic competition" anthropomorphizes state behavior without theoretical grounding

**Argumentative Resilience:**

- **Unaddressed counterargument:** If inducing an adversary to overspend on military capabilities were an effective strategy, the Soviet Union's collapse (partly attributed to arms race costs) should have eliminated the Russian threat—it manifestly did not
- The brief doesn't engage with the possibility that Russian military investment might actually _increase_ rather than decrease threat capabilities (i.e., the "security dilemma" problem)

### Vulnerability Rating: 7/10

### Improvement Path

- Provide historical case analysis of successful "cost-imposing" military strategies
- Model Russian likely responses using actual Russian defense doctrine rather than assuming symmetrical reactions
- Distinguish clearly between imposing costs and achieving strategic objectives
- Include escalation risk analysis for each military option

## Argument 3: Geopolitical Measures Can Exploit Russia's External Vulnerabilities

### Argument Summary

The brief argues that providing lethal aid to Ukraine and expanding influence in Russia's near abroad can exploit Russia's "greatest point of external vulnerability" and force resource diversion.

### Critical Analysis

**Logical Structure:**

- The argument contains a **paradox**: lethal aid to Ukraine is described as exploiting Russia's "greatest point of external vulnerability," yet the assessment table rates it only "Moderate" likelihood of success with "High" costs and risks
- The brief acknowledges that such aid must be "carefully calibrated" to avoid provoking wider conflict—this suggests the strategy operates in a narrow, unstable zone between ineffectiveness and dangerous escalation
- **Non sequitur:** The brief notes that flipping Transnistria would "save Moscow money" yet still lists it as a cost-imposing option—saving an adversary money contradicts the stated objective

**Evidentiary Foundation:**

- No historical analysis is provided of similar interventions and their effects on Russian behavior
- The assessment of "moderate" likelihood for lethal aid to Ukraine is presented without supporting analysis
- The claim that Russia's "greatest point of external vulnerability" is Ukraine is asserted rather than demonstrated

**Rhetorical Clarity:**

- The euphemism "cost-imposing" obscures the human costs of these measures, particularly for populations in contested regions
- The brief's clinical tone ("exploit vulnerabilities," "stress Russia") may obscure ethical and legal implications

**Argumentative Resilience:**

- **Unaddressed counterargument:** Near-abroad competition may actually strengthen Putin's domestic position by enabling rally-round-the-flag effects
- The brief doesn't engage with the possibility that Russia might respond to near-abroad pressure through asymmetric escalation (cyber, information operations, election interference) rather than military investment
- **Contradiction:** The brief acknowledges that "expanding ties in the South Caucasus...would be difficult because of geography and history" and that "reducing Russian influence in Central Asia would be very difficult"—yet these are still presented as viable options

### Vulnerability Rating: 8/10

### Improvement Path

- Provide analysis of Russian historical responses to near-abroad challenges
- Address the domestic politics dimension: how might these measures affect regime stability vs. regime belligerence?
- Reconcile the contradiction between describing Ukraine as the "greatest vulnerability" and assigning only moderate success likelihood

## Argument 4: Ideological/Informational Measures Can Undermine Regime Stability

### Argument Summary

The brief argues that measures to diminish faith in electoral systems, create perceptions of regime corruption, encourage domestic protests, and undermine Russia's international image can impose costs by exploiting regime anxieties about stability.

### Critical Analysis

**Logical Structure:**

- The argument assumes that **regime instability equals strategic advantage**—a questionable inference
- History suggests that authoritarian regimes under external pressure often become _more_ aggressive internationally, not less (the "diversionary war" hypothesis)
- The brief itself acknowledges this risk: "there are serious risks that the Kremlin could increase repression or lash out and pursue a diversionary conflict abroad that might run counter to Western interests"

**Evidentiary Foundation:**

- The assessments are particularly weak here—all options are rated "Low" or "Moderate" likelihood of success, with "High" costs/risks
- The brief admits "it is hard to assess whether political volatility and protests would lead to a more extended Russia"
- No evidence is provided that Western information campaigns can effectively penetrate Russia's controlled media environment

**Rhetorical Clarity:**

- The passive construction ("diminishing faith," "creating the perception") obscures agency and implementation challenges
- The brief doesn't define what "success" looks like for these measures—regime change? Policy change? Resource diversion?

**Argumentative Resilience:**

- **Unaddressed counterargument:** The 2011-2013 protests (which the brief implicitly references) didn't produce a more compliant Russia—they preceded the 2014 Crimea annexation and increased confrontation
- The brief doesn't engage with the "blowback" problem: these measures, if attributed to Western sources, can be used to delegitimize domestic opposition as foreign agents
- **Contradiction:** The brief states these measures "would be difficult for Western governments to directly increase the incidence or intensity of anti-regime activities in Russia"—yet still lists them as policy options

### Vulnerability Rating: 9/10

### Improvement Path

- Provide theoretical framework linking regime instability to strategic behavior change
- Include historical case analysis of information/ideological campaigns and their effects
- Clarify whether the objective is regime change or behavioral change, and how either serves U.S. interests
- Address the attribution problem: how to implement covert measures that won't backfire if exposed

## Argument 5: The Cost-Imposition Framework Itself Is Strategically Sound

### Argument Summary

The brief's meta-argument is that the Cold War "cost-imposition" framework—channeling competition into areas of U.S. advantage to force adversary resource diversion—is applicable to contemporary Russia and offers a coherent strategic approach.

### Critical Analysis

**Logical Structure:**

- **False analogy:** The Soviet Union was a global ideological competitor with a command economy and formal alliance structure; contemporary Russia is a territorially focused, nationalist, market-integrated (though sanctioned) state with different decision-making structures
- The brief acknowledges that "Russia is not seeking military parity with the United States"—this undermines the fundamental premise of the cost-imposition model, which assumes a parity-seeking adversary that can be induced to overextend

**Evidentiary Foundation:**

- The brief cites a "seminal 1972 RAND report" but doesn't establish that its logic applies to contemporary conditions
- No comparative analysis is provided of why Cold War frameworks should work against post-Soviet Russia
- The brief admits that "extending Russia for its own sake is not a sufficient basis in most cases to consider the options discussed here"—yet the entire analysis is organized around cost-imposition as the evaluative framework

**Rhetorical Clarity:**

- The historical framing ("long-term strategic competition developed during the Cold War") creates an impression of analytical continuity that may not be warranted
- The brief's conclusions are hedged so heavily ("must be deliberately planned and carefully calibrated," "broader context of national policy") that the actionable value is unclear

**Argumentative Resilience:**

- **Unaddressed counterargument:** The Cold War cost-imposition strategy may have contributed to Soviet collapse, but at enormous cost and risk—including multiple near-nuclear crises; the brief doesn't engage with whether these risks are acceptable for current U.S. interests
- The brief doesn't address the fundamental question: what does "success" look like? A weaker Russia? A more cooperative Russia? A Russia focused on domestic rather than international concerns? These are different objectives requiring different approaches
- **Contradiction:** The brief states that "the most promising ways to extend Russia...likely fall outside the military domain," yet the majority of options analyzed (and all the "high likelihood of success" options beyond economic measures) are military

### Vulnerability Rating: 7/10

### Improvement Path

- Explicitly justify the Cold War analogy with analysis of structural similarities/differences
- Define clear strategic objectives beyond "extending" Russia
- Address the "end state" question: what regional/international order is desired, and how do these measures achieve it?
- Reconcile the tension between military and non-military options' assessed effectiveness and their prominence in the analysis

## Executive Summary: Critical Overarching Weaknesses

### 1. **The Measurement Problem**

The brief lacks a coherent theory of how "cost-imposition" translates into desired behavioral change. Getting Russia to spend more on defense or suffer economic pain is treated as an end in itself rather than establishing how this produces strategic compliance. The causal mechanism is assumed, not demonstrated.

### 2. **The Mirror-Imaging Problem**

Throughout, the analysis assumes Russia will respond to U.S. initiatives in predictable, symmetrical ways. The brief occasionally acknowledges that Russia "might simply choose not to respond" but doesn't systematically model Russian strategic culture, doctrine, or historical patterns of response to pressure.

### 3. **The Escalation Blind Spot**

While the brief acknowledges that options are "escalatory" and would prompt "counterescalation," it treats escalation as a risk to be "calibrated" rather than a fundamental challenge to the strategy's viability. With a nuclear-armed adversary, the margin for error in such calibration may be narrower than the analysis assumes.

### 4. **The Evidence Gap**

The assessments are qualitative judgments by the authors without transparent methodology, historical case analysis, or quantitative modeling. The color-coded tables create a false impression of analytical precision.

### 5. **The Objective Ambiguity**

The brief never clearly defines what "extending Russia" is meant to achieve. A Russia weakened economically may be more desperate and dangerous; a Russia militarily overextended may be more prone to miscalculation; a Russia with destabilized domestic politics may be unpredictable. The strategic end state remains unspecified.

---

**Overall Assessment:** The brief presents a methodologically structured framework for analyzing competitive strategies, but its conclusions rest on thin empirical foundations and unexamined assumptions about adversary behavior. The hedged language of the conclusions ("must be deliberately planned," "broader context") suggests the authors recognize the limitations of cost-imposition as a standalone strategy, but the analysis doesn't adequately address these limitations.
